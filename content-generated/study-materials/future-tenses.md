# Future Tenses

**Level:** A2 | **Time:** 40-50 mins | **Category:** Grammar

---

# What are Future Tenses?

Future tenses are grammatical structures used to express actions, events, or states that will happen after the present moment. In English, we have multiple ways to talk about the future, each serving a specific purpose—whether it's making predictions, expressing plans, talking about scheduled events, or describing actions that will be in progress at a future time. Unlike Hindi, which often uses present tense forms with time markers to indicate future meaning (जैसे "मैं कल जाता हूँ" for "I will go tomorrow"), English requires specific future structures with auxiliary verbs like "will," "shall," or "be going to."

Understanding future tenses is crucial because they help us communicate our intentions, make promises, discuss schedules, and predict outcomes with precision. For Indian learners, mastering these tenses can be challenging due to language transfer from Hindi, Marathi, Tamil, or other regional languages where future constructions work differently. Many Indian languages use different grammatical patterns—for instance, Hindi combines the verb root with future tense markers (जाऊँगा, जाएगा), while English relies heavily on auxiliary verbs placed before the main verb.

In competitive exams like SSC CGL, SBI PO, IBPS, and IELTS, future tenses appear frequently in error spotting, sentence correction, fill-in-the-blanks, and writing sections. Examiners specifically test whether candidates can distinguish between "will" and "going to," use future continuous correctly, and apply future perfect tenses in complex time relationships. A single error in future tense usage can cost you valuable marks in the English language section, which often acts as a qualifying filter in banking and government job exams.

For practical communication—whether writing emails at work, making presentations, or appearing for interviews—using correct future tenses demonstrates language proficiency and clarity of thought. When Ramesh from Pune says "I will explain the project details tomorrow" versus "I am going to explain the project details tomorrow," the subtle difference in meaning (spontaneous decision versus pre-planned action) matters in professional contexts. This guide will equip you with the knowledge to use all five future tenses confidently and accurately.

# Core Concepts

### . Simple Future (Will/Shall)

**Definition:** The simple future tense expresses actions that will happen in the future, spontaneous decisions made at the moment of speaking, predictions based on opinions, promises, offers, threats, or future facts. It is formed using "will" (or "shall" in formal British English for first person) + base form of the verb.

**Rules:**

1. **Structure:** Subject + will/shall + base verb (no -s, -ing, or -ed endings)  - "I will study" NOT "I will studies" or "I will studying"

2. **Shall vs Will:** In modern English, "will" is used for all persons (I, you, he, she, we, they). "Shall" is used in formal contexts, legal documents, or when making offers/suggestions with "I" or "we" (Shall I open the window? Shall we begin?).

3. **Spontaneous decisions:** Use "will" when you decide something at the moment of speaking, not planned in advance.  - "The phone is ringing. I'll answer it." (decision made now)

4. **Predictions based on opinion:** Use "will" for predictions based on personal belief, not visible evidence.  - "I think India will win the World Cup" (opinion, not based on what you see right now)

5. **Negatives and Questions:**  - Negative: Subject + will not (won't) + base verb  - Question: Will + subject + base verb?

**Examples:**  **CORRECT:** "Priya will join ICICI Bank next month."
 **INCORRECT:** "Priya will joins ICICI Bank next month." (No -s with will)  **CORRECT:** "I think the monsoon will arrive late this year."
 **INCORRECT:** "I think the monsoon will arriving late this year." (Use base verb, not -ing)  **CORRECT:** "Don't worry, I'll help you with the SSC preparation."
 **INCORRECT:** "Don't worry, I will be help you with the SSC preparation." (No 'be' needed)  **CORRECT:** "Shall we start the meeting at 10 AM?"
 **INCORRECT:** "Shall we starting the meeting at 10 AM?" (Use base verb)  **CORRECT:** "They won't attend the wedding in Bangalore."
 **INCORRECT:** "They will not be attend the wedding in Bangalore." (No 'be' needed in simple future negative)  **CORRECT:** "Will Rahul complete his MBA before getting married?"
 **INCORRECT:** "Will Rahul completes his MBA before getting married?" (No -s after will)

### . Going To Future

**Definition:** The "going to" future expresses pre-planned intentions, decisions made before the moment of speaking, and predictions based on present evidence (something you can see or know right now). It emphasizes that you have already thought about or planned something.

**Rules:**

1. **Structure:** Subject + am/is/are + going to + base verb  - Person determines the form: I am, you/we/they are, he/she/it is

2. **Pre-planned intentions:** Use "going to" when you have already decided to do something before speaking.  - "I'm going to apply for SBI PO this year." (decision already made)

3. **Predictions based on evidence:** Use "going to" when present evidence shows something will happen.  - "Look at those dark clouds! It's going to rain." (you see the clouds now)

4. **NOT for spontaneous decisions:** Don't use "going to" for decisions made at the moment of speaking.  -  "The phone is ringing. I'm going to answer it." →  "I'll answer it."

5. **Negatives and Questions:**  - Negative: Subject + am/is/are + not + going to + base verb  - Question: Am/Is/Are + subject + going to + base verb?

**Examples (Focus on "will vs going to" confusion):**  **CORRECT:** "Anjali is going to study Engineering at IIT Delhi." (already decided/planned)
 **INCORRECT:** "Anjali will going to study Engineering at IIT Delhi." (Never mix will + going to)  **CORRECT:** "I'm going to buy a new laptop for my preparation." (already planned)
 **INCORRECT:** "I'm going buy a new laptop for my preparation." (Missing 'to')  **CORRECT:** "Watch out! That rickshaw is going to hit the car!" (present evidence—you see it happening)
 **INCORRECT:** "Watch out! That rickshaw will going hit the car!" (Never use will + going together)  **CORRECT:** "They aren't going to attend the coaching classes anymore." (planned decision)
 **INCORRECT:** "They won't going to attend the coaching classes anymore." (Don't mix won't + going to)  **CORRECT:** "Are you going to appear for UPSC this year?" (asking about plans)
 **INCORRECT:** "Will you going to appear for UPSC this year?" (Don't mix will + going to)  **CORRECT:** "He's going to fail the exam if he doesn't study." (prediction based on current behavior)
 **INCORRECT:** "He's will going to fail the exam if he doesn't study." (Never combine 's + will)

### . Future Continuous

**Definition:** The future continuous (or future progressive) tense describes actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future. It emphasizes the duration or ongoing nature of a future action. It's formed using "will be" + verb-ing.

**Rules:**

1. **Structure:** Subject + will be + verb-ing  - "I will be working" NOT "I will working" or "I am will be working"

2. **Action in progress at a specific future time:** Use when describing what will be happening at an exact moment in the future.  - "At 9 PM tonight, I will be studying for SSC." (at that exact time, the action will be ongoing)

3. **Polite inquiries about plans:** Often used to ask about someone's plans politely without imposing.  - "Will you be using the computer this evening?" (more polite than "Will you use...?")

4. **Parallel actions in the future:** When two actions will be happening simultaneously in the future.  - "While Rohan is giving his interview, Sneha will be waiting outside."

5. **Negatives and Questions:**  - Negative: Subject + will not be (won't be) + verb-ing  - Question: Will + subject + be + verb-ing?

**Examples:**  **CORRECT:** "This time next week, I will be attending the orientation at SBI."
 **INCORRECT:** "This time next week, I will attending the orientation at SBI." (Need 'be' before verb-ing)  **CORRECT:** "They will be traveling to Mumbai when we reach Delhi."
 **INCORRECT:** "They will be travel to Mumbai when we reach Delhi." (Use verb-ing, not base form)  **CORRECT:** "Will you be working late on Friday evening?"
 **INCORRECT:** "Will you be work late on Friday evening?" (Need verb-ing form)  **CORRECT:** "I won't be attending the wedding because I'll be preparing for my exam."
 **INCORRECT:** "I won't be attend the wedding because I'll preparing for my exam." (Need 'be' in second clause too)  **CORRECT:** "At 6 AM tomorrow, Kavita will be leaving for the airport."
 **INCORRECT:** "At 6 AM tomorrow, Kavita will leaving for the airport." (Need 'be')  **CORRECT:** "This evening, while you're watching TV, I will be solving mock tests."
 **INCORRECT:** "This evening, while you're watching TV, I will be solve mock tests." (Use solving, not solve)

### . Future Perfect

**Definition:** The future perfect tense describes an action that will be completed before a specific time or another action in the future. It shows that something will be finished by a certain point. It's formed using "will have" + past participle (third form of verb).

**Rules:**

1. **Structure:** Subject + will have + past participle (V3)  - "I will have completed" NOT "I will have complete" or "I will had completed"

2. **Completed before a future time:** Use when an action will be finished before a specific future moment.  - "By 2027, I will have completed my graduation." (finished before 2027 ends)

3. **Often used with time expressions:** Common with "by" (by next month, by the time, by then), "before" (before he arrives), "when" (when you reach).

4. **Never use "had" with future perfect:** "Will have" is correct; "will had" is always wrong.

5. **Negatives and Questions:**  - Negative: Subject + will not have (won't have) + past participle  - Question: Will + subject + have + past participle?

**Examples:**  **CORRECT:** "By December 2026, Rajesh will have worked at Infosys for five years."
 **INCORRECT:** "By December 2026, Rajesh will have work at Infosys for five years." (Use past participle 'worked')  **CORRECT:** "I will have finished my SSC preparation before the notification comes out."
 **INCORRECT:** "I will have finish my SSC preparation before the notification comes out." (Use 'finished', not 'finish')  **CORRECT:** "By the time you reach Pune, we will have left for Mumbai."
 **INCORRECT:** "By the time you reach Pune, we will left for Mumbai." (Need 'have' before left)  **CORRECT:** "They won't have received the results by Friday."
 **INCORRECT:** "They won't have receive the results by Friday." (Use past participle 'received')  **CORRECT:** "Will you have completed the project before the deadline?"
 **INCORRECT:** "Will you have complete the project before the deadline?" (Use 'completed')  **CORRECT:** "By 2030, India will have become a five-trillion-dollar economy." (expert prediction)
 **INCORRECT:** "By 2030, India will have became a five-trillion-dollar economy." (Use 'become', not 'became')

### . Future Perfect Continuous

**Definition:** The future perfect continuous tense describes an ongoing action that will continue up to a specific point in the future, emphasizing the duration of the activity. It's formed using "will have been" + verb-ing. This tense is less common but appears in advanced English exams.

**Rules:**

1. **Structure:** Subject + will have been + verb-ing  - "I will have been working" NOT "I will have been work" or "I will been working"

2. **Emphasizes duration up to a future point:** Use when stressing how long something will have been happening by a certain time.  - "By next month, I will have been preparing for UPSC for two years." (focus on duration: two years)

3. **Often used with duration expressions:** Common with "for" (for three hours, for five years) and "since" (since morning, since 2024).

4. **All four words are necessary:** Will + have + been + verb-ing (don't drop any part)

5. **Negatives and Questions:**  - Negative: Subject + will not have been (won't have been) + verb-ing  - Question: Will + subject + have been + verb-ing?

**Examples:**  **CORRECT:** "By the end of this year, Meera will have been teaching at DAV School for ten years."
 **INCORRECT:** "By the end of this year, Meera will have been teach at DAV School for ten years." (Use teaching, not teach)  **CORRECT:** "Next week, we will have been living in Bangalore for six months."
 **INCORRECT:** "Next week, we will been living in Bangalore for six months." (Need 'have' before been)  **CORRECT:** "By 5 PM, I will have been studying for eight hours straight."
 **INCORRECT:** "By 5 PM, I will have been study for eight hours straight." (Use studying)  **CORRECT:** "They won't have been working here for long when the office relocates."
 **INCORRECT:** "They won't have working here for long when the office relocates." (Need 'been' before working)  **CORRECT:** "Will you have been waiting for more than two hours by the time I arrive?"
 **INCORRECT:** "Will you have been wait for more than two hours by the time I arrive?" (Use waiting)  **CORRECT:** "By tomorrow, he will have been traveling for 24 hours without rest."
 **INCORRECT:** "By tomorrow, he will have been travel for 24 hours without rest." (Use traveling)

# Pro Tips for Competitive Exams

- **SSC Error Spotting:** Examiners frequently test "will + base verb" errors. Look for sentences like "He will comes" or "I will going" — the first adds unnecessary -s, the second mixes will + going to. Both are wrong. Remember: will = always base form, no exceptions.

- **Banking Cloze Tests:** When you see time markers like "by next quarter" or "before the financial year ends," the answer is likely future perfect ("will have + V3"), not simple future. Banks love testing whether you understand completion before a deadline.

- **Will vs Going To in Error Spotting:** If the sentence mentions prior planning or visible evidence ("Look at those clouds..."), "going to" is correct. If it's a spontaneous offer or opinion-based prediction ("I think..."), "will" is correct. SSC often tricks you by using the wrong one.

- **IELTS Writing Task 2:** When making predictions about the future ("In 2050, electric vehicles will dominate..."), use simple future (will + base verb) for general predictions. Avoid "going to" in academic writing unless discussing visible trends. For suggestions, use "should" or "will need to," not "will have to."

- **Never use "will" in if-clauses:** In Banking and SSC, 30% of future tense errors involve first conditional. Remember: "If it rains [present], we will cancel [future]" NOT "If it will rain, we will cancel." This is the #1 mistake Indian students make.

- **Future Continuous for politeness:** In descriptive writing or formal emails (IBPS Mains), use future continuous to sound more polite: "Will you be joining the conference?" sounds better than "Will you join the conference?" This subtle difference can improve your writing score.

- **Watch for "by the time" constructions:** When you see "by the time + subject + present tense verb" in one clause, the other clause needs future perfect. Example: "By the time she reaches [present] the station, the train will have left [future perfect]." SSC tests this pattern heavily in sentence correction.

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

## Mistake 1: Mixing "Will" and "Going To" in One Structure  **WRONG:** "Neha will going to join the coaching classes next month."  **CORRECT:** "Neha will join the coaching classes next month." OR "Neha is going to join the coaching classes next month."  **WHY:** This is the #1 error made by Hindi/regional language speakers. In Hindi, we say "वह अगले महीने कोचिंग join करेगी" using one structure. English requires you to choose: either "will + base verb" (for spontaneous/general future) OR "am/is/are going to + base verb" (for planned future). Never write "will going to" — it's grammatically impossible because you're using two different future markers together.

---

## Mistake 2: Adding -s/-es After Modal Verbs  **WRONG:** "He will attends the meeting at 3 PM."  **CORRECT:** "He will attend the meeting at 3 PM."  **WHY:** After modal verbs (will, shall, can, could, should, would, may, might, must), ALWAYS use the base form of the verb without any ending. The rule "he/she/it + verb-s" applies only to simple present tense, not to modal verbs. This error comes from over-applying present tense rules to future tense.

---

## Mistake 3: Using "Will Be" + Base Verb Instead of Verb-ing  **WRONG:** "Tomorrow at this time, I will be attend the interview."  **CORRECT:** "Tomorrow at this time, I will be attending the interview."  **WHY:** "Will be" creates future continuous tense, which MUST be followed by verb-ing form. If you want simple future, drop "be" entirely and write "I will attend." If you want to emphasize an action in progress at a specific future time, use "will be + verb-ing." Many students add "be" thinking it makes the sentence more formal, but it changes the entire tense structure.

---

## Mistake 4: Forgetting "Have" in Future Perfect  **WRONG:** "By 2028, I will complete my engineering degree."  **CORRECT:** "By 2028, I will have completed my engineering degree."  **WHY:** The word "by" signals a deadline, meaning the action will be finished BEFORE that time. This requires future perfect tense: "will have + past participle." Using simple future ("will complete") suggests the action happens AT that moment, not before it. SSC and Banking exams heavily test this distinction with time markers like "by next month," "by the time," "before the year ends."

---

## Mistake 5: Using Two Modal Verbs Together  **WRONG:** "She will can solve this problem easily."  **CORRECT:** "She will be able to solve this problem easily." OR "She can solve this problem easily."  **WHY:** English grammar never allows two modal verbs (will, can, should, must, etc.) in sequence. The sentence structure breaks down because both are auxiliary verbs trying to modify the same main verb. When combining "will" with the concept of "can," replace "can" with "be able to," which is not a modal and can work with "will."

---

## Mistake 6: Missing "To" in "Going To" Structure  **WRONG:** "I am going visit my relatives in Kerala next month."  **CORRECT:** "I am going to visit my relatives in Kerala next month."  **WHY:** "Going to" is a fixed phrasal structure for expressing planned future actions. You cannot skip "to" — it's an integral part of the future form. Hindi speakers often drop "to" because Hindi future doesn't require an equivalent connector (जा रहा हूँ doesn't need "to"). Always write "am/is/are going TO + base verb" as a complete unit.

---

## Mistake 7: Wrong Word Order in Questions  **WRONG:** "When you will start preparing for UPSC?"  **CORRECT:** "When will you start preparing for UPSC?"  **WHY:** In English questions with question words (what, when, where, why, how), the structure is: Question word + auxiliary verb (will) + subject + main verb. Indian languages often keep subject-before-verb order in questions (तुम कब शुरू करोगे?), but English inverts the word order. This is a frequent error in SSC descriptive tests and IELTS speaking.

---

## Mistake 8: Using "Will" in If-Clauses (First Conditional Error)  **WRONG:** "If it will rain tomorrow, we will postpone the trip."  **CORRECT:** "If it rains tomorrow, we will postpone the trip."  **WHY:** In first conditional sentences (expressing real future possibilities), the if-clause uses simple present tense, NOT future tense. Only the main clause (result clause) uses "will." This is one of the most tested grammar points in SSC CGL and Banking exams. Remember the formula: If + present simple, will + base verb. Hindi uses future in both clauses (अगर बारिश होगी तो...), causing this error.

---

## Mistake 9: Confusing Future Simple and Future Continuous Time Frames  **WRONG:** "I will study when you will call me tonight." (using simple future for simultaneous action)  **CORRECT:** "I will be studying when you call me tonight."  **WHY:** When describing an action that will be in progress (ongoing) at the time another action happens, use future continuous (will be + verb-ing) for the longer action. The time clause (when/while/as) uses simple present, not future. The correct structure emphasizes that studying will already be happening when the call comes. Also note: after "when," use present tense, not "will call."

---

## Mistake 10: Using Future Perfect Without Time Reference  **WRONG:** "I will have finished my homework." (incomplete sentence without time marker)  **CORRECT:** "I will have finished my homework by 8 PM." OR "I will have finished my homework before dinner."  **WHY:** Future perfect tense MUST include a time reference point (by + time, before + event, when + clause) to show when the action will be completed. Without this context, the sentence feels incomplete. Simple future ("I will finish my homework") is better if you're not specifying a deadline. In exams, if you see "by," "before," or "when" followed by another future event, future perfect is almost always required.

# Quick Practice (10 Questions)

**Q1.** Choose the correct sentence:

a) Ramesh will joins HDFC Bank after his MBA.

b) Ramesh will join HDFC Bank after his MBA.

c) Ramesh will be join HDFC Bank after his MBA.

d) Ramesh will joining HDFC Bank after his MBA.

**Answer:** b) - After modal verbs like "will," always use the base form of the verb without adding -s, -ing, or any other ending. Option (a) incorrectly adds -s, option (c) incorrectly adds "be," and option (d) uses -ing form, all of which violate the rule: will + base verb.

---

**Q2.** Look at those dark clouds! It ___ rain heavily in a few minutes.

a) will

b) is going to

c) will be

d) was going to

**Answer:** b) - When making predictions based on present evidence (you can see the dark clouds right now), use "is going to," not "will." "Will" is used for opinions or spontaneous decisions without visible proof. The clouds are observable evidence that rain is imminent, making "going to" the correct choice.

---

**Q3.** By the time you reach the coaching center, the class ___ already ___.

a) will / start

b) will / started

c) will have / started

d) will be / starting

**Answer:** c) - "By the time" indicates that one action (class starting) will be completed before another action (you reaching). This requires future perfect tense: will have + past participle. Options (a) and (b) use incorrect tense forms, while (d) uses future continuous, which doesn't show completion before the second event.

---

**Q4.** If the notification ___ next week, I ___ apply immediately.

a) will come / will

b) comes / will

c) will come / would

d) came / will

**Answer:** b) - In first conditional sentences (real future possibilities), the if-clause uses simple present tense ("comes"), and the main clause uses future ("will apply"). Never use "will" in both clauses. This is a classic SSC/Banking error spotting trap. Remember: If + present, will + base verb.

---

**Q5.** This time tomorrow, Priya ___ for her IELTS speaking test.

a) will prepare

b) will be preparing

c) will have prepared

d) is preparing

**Answer:** b) - "This time tomorrow" indicates a specific moment in the future, and the sentence describes what will be happening (in progress) at that exact time. This requires future continuous: will be + verb-ing. Option (a) is simple future (doesn't emphasize ongoing action), (c) is future perfect (for completed actions), and (d) is present continuous (for current actions).

---

**Q6.** We ___ in Bangalore for five years by next December.

a) will live

b) will be living

c) will have been living

d) are living

**Answer:** c) - "For five years by next December" emphasizes the duration of an action up to a specific future point. This requires future perfect continuous: will have been + verb-ing. Option (a) is simple future (doesn't show duration), (b) is future continuous (doesn't emphasize "by" completion point), and (d) is present continuous (wrong tense entirely).

---

**Q7.** Don't worry about dinner. I ___ cook something when I get home.

a) am going to

b) will

c) will be

d) am

**Answer:** b) - This is a spontaneous decision made at the moment of speaking (responding to someone's worry). Spontaneous decisions require "will," not "going to." If the speaker had already planned to cook before this conversation, "am going to" would be correct. But here, the decision is made instantly as a response.

---

**Q8.** Which sentence is INCORRECT?

a) Shall I help you with those bags?

b) I will going to visit Jaipur next month.

c) The train will arrive at platform 5.

d) They won't be attending the wedding.

**Answer:** b) - "I will going to" mixes two different future structures together, which is grammatically impossible. You must choose either "I will visit" (simple future) OR "I am going to visit" (planned future). Never combine "will" and "going to" in the same structure. This is the most common mistake among Indian English learners.

---

**Q9.** By 2028, India's economy ___ significantly.

a) will grow

b) will be growing

c) will have grown

d) will grew

**Answer:** c) - "By 2028" indicates a deadline, meaning the growth will be completed before 2028 ends. This requires future perfect: will have + past participle. Option (a) suggests the action happens AT that moment (not before), (b) suggests ongoing action at that time, and (d) uses wrong verb form ("grew" is simple past, not past participle; even then, structure would be wrong).

---

**Q10.** ___ you ___ the office tomorrow at 11 AM? I need to discuss something.

a) Will / be using

b) Are / will use

c) Will / use

d) Do / will be using

**Answer:** a) - This is a polite inquiry about someone's plans at a specific future time. "Will you be using" (future continuous in question form) sounds more polite and less direct than "Will you use" (simple future). The speaker is asking if the office will be occupied/in use at that specific time. Option (b) has incorrect structure, (c) is less polite but grammatically acceptable, and (d) mixes present and future incorrectly.

# Summary & Next Steps

Mastering future tenses is a critical milestone in your English language journey, especially for competitive exams like SSC CGL, Banking (SBI PO, IBPS), and IELTS. You've now learned five distinct future structures: Simple Future (will + base verb) for spontaneous decisions and predictions; Going To Future for pre-planned intentions and evidence-based predictions; Future Continuous (will be + verb-ing) for actions in progress at specific future times; Future Perfect (will have + past participle) for actions completed before future deadlines; and Future Perfect Continuous (will have been + verb-ing) for emphasizing duration up to a future point. Each tense serves a unique purpose, and knowing when to use which one gives you precision and confidence in both written and spoken English.

The most common errors among Indian learners stem from language transfer—mixing "will" and "going to," adding unnecessary -s after modal verbs, using "will" in if-clauses, and forgetting time markers with future perfect. By actively practicing the correction patterns shown in this guide and consciously avoiding Hindi/regional language interference, you'll eliminate 90% of future tense errors in your exams. Remember that competitive exam setters specifically target these predictable mistakes in error spotting and sentence correction questions. Every time you practice, focus on the "why" behind each correction—understanding the grammar rule is more powerful than memorizing isolated examples.

Future tenses don't exist in isolation; they connect to other grammar areas you need to master. Next, study **conditional sentences** (especially first, second, and third conditionals) which heavily use future forms in their result clauses. Then move to **time clauses** (when, while, before, after, as soon as, by the time) which govern whether you use present or future tenses. Finally, explore **modal verbs** (can, could, should, must, might, may) for expressing possibility, ability, obligation, and advice—many of which combine with future time contexts. Strengthening these interconnected topics will make your English grammar rock-solid for any competitive exam.
