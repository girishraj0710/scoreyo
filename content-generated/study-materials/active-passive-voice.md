# Active and Passive Voice

**Level:** B1 | **Time:** 45-55 mins | **Category:** Grammar

---

# What is Active and Passive Voice?

Voice in English grammar refers to the relationship between the subject of a sentence and the action performed. There are two voices: **Active Voice**, where the subject performs the action, and **Passive Voice**, where the subject receives the action. Understanding and using both voices correctly is crucial for competitive exams, formal writing, and effective communication.

In Active Voice, the sentence structure follows the natural subject-verb-object pattern: "The manager **approved** the proposal." The focus is on WHO did the action (the manager). In Passive Voice, the structure reverses to emphasize WHAT received the action: "The proposal **was approved** by the manager." The focus shifts to the proposal (the receiver of the action), and the doer (manager) becomes secondary or is omitted entirely.

For Indian learners, passive voice can be particularly confusing because Hindi and other regional languages structure passive sentences differently. In Hindi, we say "प्रस्ताव स्वीकृत किया गया" (Prastav sweekrith kiya gaya), which translates awkwardly to "Proposal approved was done." English passive voice requires auxiliary verbs (is, was, has been) + past participle, a structure that doesn't map directly to Hindi grammar. Additionally, Indian English speakers often overuse passive voice in formal contexts, believing it sounds more professional, when active voice would be clearer and stronger.

Passive voice is NOT inherently wrong—it serves specific purposes: emphasizing the action or result over the doer, writing scientific reports where the researcher is irrelevant ("The experiment was conducted"), or describing situations where the doer is unknown ("My phone was stolen"). However, excessive passive voice makes writing weak, vague, and bureaucratic. Competitive exams like SSC, Banking, IELTS, and UPSC test whether you can identify passive constructions, convert between active and passive, and choose the appropriate voice for context.

In SSC CGL and Banking exams, voice-related questions appear in three forms: direct active-passive conversion (5-7 marks), error spotting with incorrect passive structures (3-5 marks), and sentence improvement where passive weakens clarity (3-5 marks). IELTS Writing rewards varied sentence structures, including passive voice used appropriately—but penalizes overuse or incorrect auxiliary verb forms. Mastering active and passive voice isn't just about grammar rules; it's about strategic communication—knowing when to emphasize the doer (active) and when to emphasize the action or result (passive).

# Core Concepts

### Active Voice: Subject Performs the Action

**Definition:** In active voice, the subject of the sentence performs the action expressed by the verb. The structure is straightforward: Subject → Verb → Object.

**Structure:** Subject + Verb + Object

**Rules:**

1. The subject comes first and performs the action.
2. The verb clearly shows what the subject is doing.
3. The object (if present) receives the action.
4. Active voice is direct, clear, and strong.

**Examples:**  **ACTIVE:** "Rahul **wrote** the report."
- Subject: Rahul (doer)
- Verb: wrote (action)
- Object: the report (receiver)  **ACTIVE:** "The Reserve Bank of India **announced** new interest rates."
- Subject: The Reserve Bank of India
- Verb: announced
- Object: new interest rates  **ACTIVE:** "Students **solved** all the practice questions."
- Subject: Students
- Verb: solved
- Object: all the practice questions

**When to Use Active Voice:**

- When the doer is important or interesting: "Steve Jobs **founded** Apple."
- When you want direct, clear, strong writing: "The committee **rejected** the proposal."
- In instructions or commands: "Complete the form and submit it by Friday."
- In most everyday communication: "I **bought** a new laptop."

---

### Passive Voice: Subject Receives the Action

**Definition:** In passive voice, the subject of the sentence receives the action expressed by the verb. The doer of the action (agent) is either introduced by "by" or omitted entirely. The structure is: Subject + Auxiliary Verb (be) + Past Participle + (by Agent).

**Structure:** Subject + is/am/are/was/were/has been/have been/will be + Past Participle (+ by Agent)

**Rules:**

1. The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.
2. The verb changes to: auxiliary verb (form of "be") + past participle.
3. The original subject (doer) either becomes "by + agent" or is omitted.
4. Passive voice emphasizes the action or the receiver, not the doer.

**Formation Steps:**

**Active:** "Rahul wrote the report."
**Passive:** "The report **was written** by Rahul."

Step-by-step:
1. Object of active sentence (the report) → Subject of passive sentence
2. Verb changes: wrote → was written (auxiliary "was" + past participle "written")
3. Original subject (Rahul) → "by Rahul" (optional)

**Examples:**  **PASSIVE:** "The report **was written** by Rahul."
- Subject: The report (receiver)
- Verb: was written (auxiliary + past participle)
- Agent: by Rahul (doer)  **PASSIVE:** "New interest rates **were announced** by the Reserve Bank of India."
- Subject: New interest rates
- Verb: were announced
- Agent: by the Reserve Bank of India  **PASSIVE:** "All the practice questions **were solved** by students."
- Subject: All the practice questions
- Verb: were solved
- Agent: by students

**When to Use Passive Voice:**

- When the doer is unknown or unimportant: "My phone **was stolen**." (You don't know who stole it.)
- When the action or result is more important than the doer: "The Taj Mahal **was built** in the 17th century." (Focus on the monument, not the builders.)
- In scientific or technical writing: "The experiment **was conducted** at room temperature." (The researcher is irrelevant.)
- When you want to sound formal or diplomatic: "Mistakes **were made**." (Avoids directly blaming someone.)

### Active to Passive Conversion: Complete Tense Chart

This is the MOST tested concept in SSC and Banking exams. You must memorize how each tense converts from active to passive.

| Tense | Active Voice | Passive Voice | Auxiliary Pattern |
|-------|--------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Present Simple** | Priya **writes** reports. | Reports **are written** by Priya. | am/is/are + past participle |
| **Present Continuous** | Priya **is writing** a report. | A report **is being written** by Priya. | am/is/are being + past participle |
| **Present Perfect** | Priya **has written** the report. | The report **has been written** by Priya. | has/have been + past participle |
| **Past Simple** | Priya **wrote** the report. | The report **was written** by Priya. | was/were + past participle |
| **Past Continuous** | Priya **was writing** the report. | The report **was being written** by Priya. | was/were being + past participle |
| **Past Perfect** | Priya **had written** the report. | The report **had been written** by Priya. | had been + past participle |
| **Future Simple (will)** | Priya **will write** the report. | The report **will be written** by Priya. | will be + past participle |
| **Future Perfect** | Priya **will have written** the report. | The report **will have been written** by Priya. | will have been + past participle |
| **Present Perfect Continuous** | Priya **has been writing** reports. | (Rarely used in passive) | has/have been being + past participle (awkward, avoid) |
| **Past Perfect Continuous** | Priya **had been writing** reports. | (Rarely used in passive) | had been being + past participle (awkward, avoid) |
| **Future Continuous** | Priya **will be writing** the report. | (Rarely used in passive) | will be being + past participle (awkward, avoid) |
| **Modals (can, should, must)** | You **can solve** this. | This **can be solved** by you. | modal + be + past participle |

**Key Observations:**

- Perfect continuous tenses (present perfect continuous, past perfect continuous) and future continuous rarely convert to passive because the structures become awkward and unnatural ("has been being written"). Exams don't test these.
- Modal verbs (can, could, should, would, must, may, might) use a simple pattern in passive: **modal + be + past participle**.  - Active: "You should complete the form."  - Passive: "The form should be completed."

### Special Cases in Active-Passive Conversion

**Case 1: Sentences with Two Objects (Direct and Indirect)**

Some sentences have two objects—an indirect object (person) and a direct object (thing). You can form passive voice by making EITHER object the subject.

**Active:** "The teacher gave **Amit** (indirect object) **a prize** (direct object)."

**Passive Option 1:** "**Amit** was given a prize by the teacher." (Indirect object becomes subject—more common)

**Passive Option 2:** "**A prize** was given to Amit by the teacher." (Direct object becomes subject—less common, needs "to")

**Active:** "She sent **me** (indirect object) **an email** (direct object)."

**Passive Option 1:** "**I** was sent an email by her."

**Passive Option 2:** "**An email** was sent to me by her."

---

**Case 2: Questions (Active to Passive)**

When converting questions from active to passive, the question word stays at the beginning, and the passive structure follows.

**Active:** "Who wrote this book?"
**Passive:** "By whom was this book written?" (Formal) OR "Who was this book written by?" (Informal, more natural)

**Active:** "Did Priya solve the problem?"
**Passive:** "Was the problem solved by Priya?"

**Active:** "Where did they hold the meeting?"
**Passive:** "Where was the meeting held?"

---

**Case 3: Imperative Sentences (Commands/Requests)**

Imperative sentences (commands or requests) in active voice convert to passive using **"Let" + object + "be" + past participle**.

**Active:** "Close the door."
**Passive:** "Let the door be closed."

**Active:** "Help the poor."
**Passive:** "Let the poor be helped."

**Active:** "Do not touch this wire."
**Passive:** "Let this wire not be touched." OR "This wire should not be touched."

---

**Case 4: Negative Sentences**

In negative sentences, "not" appears after the auxiliary verb in passive voice.

**Active:** "Rahul did not write the report."
**Passive:** "The report was not written by Rahul."

**Active:** "They have not completed the project."
**Passive:** "The project has not been completed by them."

---

**Case 5: Sentences with Modal Verbs**

Modal verbs (can, could, should, would, must, may, might, will, shall) follow a fixed passive pattern: **modal + be + past participle**.

**Active:** "You should complete the assignment."
**Passive:** "The assignment should be completed."

**Active:** "They must submit the form by Friday."
**Passive:** "The form must be submitted by Friday."

**Active:** "We can solve this problem."
**Passive:** "This problem can be solved."

---

### When to Omit "By + Agent" in Passive Voice

In passive voice, the doer (agent) is introduced by "by." However, you can and SHOULD omit "by + agent" in these situations:

**Omit when the agent is obvious, unimportant, or unknown:**  **GOOD:** "The Taj Mahal was built in the 17th century." (No need to mention the builders—everyone knows or doesn't care who built it.)  **AWKWARD:** "The Taj Mahal was built by Shah Jahan's architects and workers in the 17th century." (Unnecessarily wordy.)  **GOOD:** "My phone was stolen." (You don't know who stole it.)  **AWKWARD:** "My phone was stolen by someone." (Stating the obvious—of course someone stole it.)  **GOOD:** "Mistakes were made." (Diplomatic, avoids blaming.)  **DIRECT:** "Mistakes were made by the manager." (Too direct, less diplomatic.)

**Keep "by + agent" when the doer is important, surprising, or adds value:**  **GOOD:** "The theory of relativity was developed **by Albert Einstein**." (Einstein is important—knowing the creator adds value.)  **GOOD:** "This novel was written **by a 12-year-old**." (Surprising fact—age matters.)  **WEAK:** "This novel was written." (Missing important context—WHO wrote it is surprising.)
